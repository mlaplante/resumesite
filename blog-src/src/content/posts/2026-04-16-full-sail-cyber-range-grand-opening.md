There are moments that remind you why you stay connected to the places that shaped you. Today was one of those. I was at Full Sail University for the grand opening of their brand-new cyber range, and walking through that facility, I couldn't help but think about how different the landscape is from when I was a student there.

## What the Cyber Range Brings

For anyone unfamiliar, a cyber range is essentially a controlled environment where students can practice offensive and defensive security techniques against realistic infrastructure — without the consequences of doing it in production. Think simulated networks, vulnerable machines, active threat scenarios, and red team/blue team exercises all running in a sandboxed environment.

Full Sail's new range is purpose-built for their students, and it's impressive. We're talking about an environment where students can practice incident response, run penetration tests, analyze malware, and defend live infrastructure — all before they ever step foot in a SOC or touch a production system. That kind of hands-on experience is invaluable, and frankly, it's the kind of thing that separates candidates who can talk about security from those who can actually do it.

## Why Hands-On Matters

Cybersecurity is one of those fields where theoretical knowledge only gets you so far. You can memorize the MITRE ATT&CK framework, recite the OWASP Top 10, and pass every certification exam — but if you've never actually triaged an alert, pivoted through a compromised network, or written a detection rule under pressure, there's a gap. Employers know it, and hiring managers feel it in interviews.

Having a cyber range available to students closes that gap before they graduate. They get to make mistakes in a safe environment. They get to feel the adrenaline of a simulated breach. They learn that security isn't just about knowing what to do — it's about doing it under pressure, with incomplete information, on a clock.

## Giving Back as a Hall of Fame Member

Being inducted into Full Sail's Hall of Fame wasn't just an honor — it was a commitment. For me, that means showing up. It means being in the room when they open a new facility like this, talking to students, answering the questions that don't come up in lectures, and reinforcing that the industry needs them.

I've been fortunate in my career to work across web development, cloud infrastructure, and security. Every one of those paths started with someone giving me a chance to get my hands dirty and learn by doing. The least I can do is make sure the next generation has even better opportunities than I did.

## The Bigger Picture

The cybersecurity talent shortage isn't a future problem — it's a now problem. Every organization I've worked with is struggling to find qualified security professionals. Facilities like this cyber range are part of the solution. They produce graduates who aren't just certified — they're capable. They've already been in the trenches, even if those trenches were simulated.

Full Sail continues to invest in giving students real-world tools and environments, and as someone who benefited from that philosophy firsthand, it's genuinely exciting to see it evolve. If you're a student reading this — take advantage of every second you get in that range. Break things. Fix them. Break them again. That's how you learn.

And if you're an industry professional in a position to mentor, sponsor, or support programs like this — do it. The talent pipeline doesn't fill itself.
